Diving into the Great Barrier Reef

The Great Barrier Reef
Under Pressure, Still Fighting.

Australia's Great Barrier Reef (GBR) has endured repeated mass bleachings, a coral low of 13%, and a $4 billion economic collapse. Yet 2022 brought its highest coral cover since 1985. This is how it got there, and what it will take to stay.

02 — Three Decades of Change

A Declining Reef, Then a Fragile Rebound

A cycle of disruption and incomplete recovery

Crown-of-Thorns Starfish (COTS) are large native predators that eat coral. At low densities they are a natural part of the reef. During outbreaks, a single starfish can consume up to 6 square metres of coral per year, devastating entire reef sections before moving on.

Cover fell from 26% in 1993 to a record low of 13.9% by 2012. Three forces drove this decline.

The 1998 and 2002 bleachings stripped the northern reef. Cyclone Larry (2006) and Cyclone Yasi (2011) devastated the central sections. A COTS outbreak peak in 2012 then compounded an already weakened reef.

A decade of calm allowed recovery. Cover reached 33% by 2022, the highest since surveys began. Then the 2024 bleaching, the most extensive on record, erased a decade of gains in a single year, pulling cover back to 24%.

03 — The Current State

Recovery is Real, But Uneven

Coral cover divides sharply by region

NRM Regions (Natural Resource Management) are Queensland's six coastal catchment zones, each responsible for managing land use, water quality, and marine activities for their adjacent section of reef.

Burdekin and Wet Tropics record the lowest coral cover, falling to around 7% in 2020. Both sit downstream of Queensland's most intensive agricultural catchments, where decades of fertiliser runoff have weakened reef resilience. In contrast, Burnett Mary and Fitzroy, with lower catchment pressure and less frequent thermal stress, sustain cover consistently above 40%.

The pattern is visible on the map: a gradient of green in the south, red in the centre. This is not random; rather, it directly follows the quality of the land draining into the sea beside each reef section.

COTS and coral move in opposite directions

Where COTS density is highest, coral cover is lowest, and the same regions drive both trends. In Burdekin, COTS density peaked above 2.3 per tow while coral cover sat near its record low. In Mackay Whitsunday, density reached 2.1 per tow in 2022 even as coral cover held in the mid-30s, a warning that sustained outbreaks will erode that buffer.

Burnett Mary shows the inverse: near-zero COTS throughout the survey period, alongside the highest sustained coral cover of any region. The bar charts alongside the map make this relationship explicit. Toggle through the years to see how closely the two tracks mirror each other across regions.

04 — The Ocean is Changing

Warming Waters, Toxic Runoff

Two stressors, one reef

Sea Surface Temperature (SST) anomaly measures how much warmer the ocean is than its long-term average for that location and month. Coral bleaching typically begins when the anomaly exceeds 1°C for several weeks. Chlorophyll-a is a measure of microscopic algae in the water, used as a proxy for agricultural nutrient runoff. Higher concentrations indicate more runoff reaching the reef.

Coral bleaching is triggered when sea surface temperatures exceed the local maximum by 1–2°C for several weeks. But heat alone does not tell the full story. Agricultural runoff from Queensland's catchments floods inshore reefs with excess nutrients, measured as Chlorophyll-a concentration, weakening corals before the heat even arrives.

What to look for

The two maps compare 2003 (baseline) and 2021 across every month, revealing the evolution of these stressors.

On the temperature map, cells shift from dark-cool to orange-yellow with increasing heat. Summer months run hotter; the northern GBR is consistently warmer. By 2021, every month is noticeably more orange than 2003.

On the Chlorophyll-a map, deep greens hug the coastline, the fingerprint of catchment runoff. A slight coastal increase from 2003 to 2021 is most visible in February and March.

Compounding is what kills reefs

Offshore reefs carry the highest thermal stress, with peak SST anomalies averaging +0.85°C, yet their mean coral cover is 29%, sustained by clean, nutrient-poor water.

Inshore reefs face lower thermal stress (+0.43°C) but the highest Chlorophyll-a loads (0.61 mg/m³). Nutrient stress alone suppresses coral recruitment and resilience.

Mid-shelf reefs, moderate on both axes, hold the highest mean coral cover at 34.9%. It is the combination of heat and runoff that causes the deepest damage, not either stressor alone.

05 — The Economic Reef

A $7.9 Billion Economy, and a Fragile One

Tourism dominates. Everything else is negligible.

Commercial tourism is the reef's economic engine, peaking at $6.4 billion in 2018, over 80% of total revenue. Recreational non-fishing adds around $1.1 billion at its peak. Commercial fisheries contribute roughly $200 million.

Charter fishing is barely visible even when highlighted, peaking at just $40 million, less than 1% of total revenue. It is a reminder of how completely this economy depends on a single industry.

COVID-19 border closures in 2019 exposed that dependency. Total revenue collapsed from $7.9 billion to $3.3 billion in a single year, almost entirely the loss of tourism. Employment in marine tourism fell from 40,800 to 18,000 jobs, while recreational and fishing sectors barely changed.

COVID closed the reef to tourists, and the reef recovered

The most striking pattern is counterintuitive: the years with the fewest visitors produced the fastest growth in coral cover. With international tourism gone, the reef faced less boat traffic, anchor damage, and pollution pressure. Cover climbed from 20% in 2020 to 33% by 2022, the highest since surveys began.

The 2016–17 bleaching events tell the opposite story: coral dropped to 17% as visitors simultaneously peaked near 2.6 million. Then COVID decoupled the two lines entirely. Visitors collapsed while coral surged. The 2022–23 rebound shows both recovering in step, but 2024 bleaching has since reversed much of the coral gain.

06 — Australians and Their Reef

How Queenslanders Use the Reef They Depend On

Wildlife watching unites every community

Wildlife watching is the top activity in every region, from 83% in Burdekin to a unanimous 100% in Cape York. It confirms that the reef's ecological value, its fish, turtles, and coral, underpins public connection to it everywhere. Cape York also leads in offshore fishing at 78.6%, far above any other region, reflecting a community genuinely dependent on a healthy reef.

Burnett Mary fishes least, and its reef shows it

Burnett Mary has the lowest offshore fishing (39.4%) and shore fishing (40.4%) rates of any region. This directly echoes section 03: Burnett Mary also records the highest sustained coral cover. Less fishing pressure means fewer anchors, less disturbance, and more intact reef structure. Wet Tropics shows the opposite pattern, with the highest boating hire at 48.7%, the most tourism-dependent profile, consistent with the reef degradation seen in that region. 07 — Perception, Values and Action

How Australians Perceive, Value and Protect the Reef

Chart 7a — Filtered Bar Chart
Perceived Threat Severity: Visitors vs. Non-Visitors
Mean threat score (out of 10) from SELTMP 2025 (n=2,208). Use the dropdown to compare reef visitors and non-visitors. Threats sorted by overall severity.

Non-visitors alarm more broadly. Visitors focus differently.

People who have never visited the reef consistently score most threats higher, particularly Coral Bleaching (4.49 vs 4.03), Mining (4.23 vs 3.58), and Agricultural Runoff (4.34 vs 3.98). Without direct experience, threat perception is shaped by media and general environmental concern, which tends to amplify distant and visible threats.

Visitors show a more calibrated spread. Their scores for Coral Bleaching and Agricultural Runoff drop relative to non-visitors, not because they care less, but because they place them in context alongside threats like Crown-of-Thorns Starfish, which they have likely encountered directly.

The one reversal: Coastal Development scores slightly higher among visitors (4.05 vs 3.99), a pattern consistent with first-hand observation of reef-adjacent construction.

Chart 7b — Dumbbell Chart
How We Value the Reef: Visitors vs. Non-Visitors
Mean score (1–10) per value category from SELTMP 2025. Teal = Has Visited; Grey = Never Visited. Hover each dot for the exact score.

Visiting the reef raises every value, except one

Across five of six categories, visitors score reef values higher than non-visitors. The gap is widest for Recreation (+0.83) and Economic Value (+0.53), values most directly felt through personal experience. Biodiversity scores highest overall (9.34 for visitors), reflecting near-universal recognition of the reef's ecological uniqueness.

The exception is Indigenous Heritage: non-visitors actually score it higher (7.22 vs 6.66). This likely reflects how Indigenous connection to the reef is communicated through education and media rather than on-country experience, meaning those who haven't visited may have a stronger abstract awareness of this value.

Chart 7c — Matrix Heatmap
Does Visiting the Reef Drive Protective Action?
Survey respondents (n=2,208) by visit frequency (rows) and self-reported conservation action (columns). Darker = more respondents. Formal action = volunteering or policy submissions. Independent action = donations, behaviour change.

Frequent visitors act. Everyone else mostly doesn't.

"No action taken" dominates at every visit frequency, consistent with the well-documented value-action gap. But the pattern breaks clearly at the high-frequency end. Among people who visit more than once a week, only 23% report no action, compared to 82% of those who have never visited. Formal action (volunteering, policy submissions) is almost entirely concentrated in this most-engaged group.

Independent action, such as reducing plastic use or making donations, is more evenly distributed and rises steadily with visit frequency. Casual visitors can be nudged toward independent action; converting non-visitors to formal advocates requires a deeper engagement pathway that begins with getting them to the reef.

Conclusion

The Great Barrier Reef. Still Worth Fighting For.

The Great Barrier Reef is not simply declining. It is caught in an accelerating cycle of disturbance and incomplete recovery. This story has three interlocking threads: what is happening to the reef, what it costs us, and whether Australians are ready to act.

The Ecology

The reef recovered to its highest coral cover since 1985 in 2022, then lost much of it in a single bleaching year. Most species groups are in poor condition. The window between disturbances is closing faster than the reef can heal. What happens next depends on whether the stressors driving that cycle can be reduced.

The Economy

A $7.9 billion economy built almost entirely on tourism is one bad bleaching season away from collapse. COVID showed both the vulnerability and a counterintuitive truth: when human pressure drops, coral recovers. The reef's economic future and ecological future are the same future.

The People

Australians who visit the reef value it more and act to protect it more. Those who haven't are not indifferent, they are uninformed. The path from concern to action runs through experience. Getting more people to the reef, and keeping the reef worth visiting, are the same goal.

"The reef has survived repeated bleachings, a global pandemic, and thirty years of mounting pressure, and still recovered when given the chance. The question is whether we will keep giving it that chance."
